# Lester Bangs Reviews Sarà: "The Machine Heart and the Venetian Gutter"

## By Lester Bangs

Listen, I’m gonna tell you something right now that’s gonna make all you high-society New York Review of Books types
choke on your expensive sherry. I just read this book Sarà by some dame named Calliope Tweney, and it’s the most
beautiful, technical, soul-crushing piece of architecture I’ve seen since Lou Reed decided to start a fight with his own
guitar.

The word on the street is she used a computer to help write it. A "crutch," they call it. To which I say: Who gives a
damn? You think Keith Richards’ fingers are 100% organic? You think the sound coming out of a Moog synthesizer is any
less "real" than a catgut violin? It’s all just stuff, man. It’s what you do with the stuff that counts. And what Tweney
does with the "stuff" is build a city that smells like diesel and cooked onions and rotting history, and then she puts a
broad in it who’s got wings that feel like a sack of wet laundry.

That’s the "grit," people. That’s the soul.

The main guy, William Jane, is this twitchy American writer in Venice who’s so obsessed with "receipts" and "technical
density" that he can’t even sit in a café without moving it thirty meters in his head just for the "light." He’s a total
head-case, a neurodivergent disaster area who treats his own life like a legacy code that needs a patch. I love this
guy. He’s me on a Tuesday morning after three days of Robo and no sleep, trying to figure out why the world
doesn't line up with the words I’m typing.

But then there’s Chiara. She’s the bookbinder. She’s the one who actually does the work. She’s got these "small
Wednesday disciplines" and she drinks her ombra standing up at the counter and she handles goatskins like she’s trying
to read the secrets of the universe in the grain of the leather. She’s the "Grit." She’s the part of the record that
isn't overdubbed.

And the "AI question"? It’s just noise. It’s the hiss between the tracks.
The critics are all worried that the machine is gonna take over. They think if you use a "system," you lose the "vibe."
But Tweney uses the system to describe the system. She’s writing about a world that’s constantly "updating," a world
where the supernatural is just another administrative burden you gotta shower off before you go to bed. That’s not
science fiction, man. That’s real life. We’re all just "updating." We’re all just waiting for the "System Sync" to tell
us who we’re supposed to love today.

When Chiara looks at Lamia and says _Ancora ti_ -- "Still you" -- that’s the whole ballgame. That’s the hook that gets stuck in
your head and won't let you sleep. It’s the recognition that despite all the "Updates," despite the machine, despite the
wings and the desert and the nine years of silence, the heart is still running the same recursive loop.
Is it "studied"? Maybe. Is it "pretentious"? Probably. But it’s got weight. It’s a hand-bound volume in a world of cheap
paperbacks. It’s a "hard punctuation" word at the end of a long, rambling sentence.

Punto. Basta. Amen.

The story is closed. Now go buy the record and turn it up until your ears bleed.

### Author's Note
