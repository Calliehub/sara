# SARÀ

*A novel*

---

## ONE

The Giudecca class ends at seven. I bring the women up out of *savasana* the way I always do, with the low voice and the slow count, and watch eight ribcages rise and fall in the half-dark of the studio. They are good women. They are tired. Half of them have come from work; the other half have come from the small private griefs of Venetian late autumn. The light through the high windows is the dark blue light of a city that lives on water in November.

I have been teaching this class for nine years. I have been in Venice for one hundred and fifty-two of them. I have been Lamia for longer than that, longer than the language any of these women speak, longer than the building we are in, longer than the religion that built it.

The man on the back mat is the one for tonight.

I felt him when he came up the stairs — the small hot current that runs under my skin when the right kind of man enters the room. It is not desire. It is something older. The body has been doing this work since before there were Romans on this lagoon, and the body knows its meal when the meal walks in.

He is tall, mid-forties, dark hair going silver at the temples. American. Expensive watch. The kind of man who pays for what he wants and has stopped, somewhere along the way, asking whether what he wants is what he should have. He has not been on a mat in years. He came tonight because someone gave him this address. He came tonight because he is in Venice alone and he is looking for something and he has decided that something is me.

His name is Edward Marsh. I know this without having been told. I have been doing this long enough that the file assembles itself when the door opens.

I do not tell the women that one of them is on his way out. They roll their mats. They drift toward the door. Carla kisses me on each cheek, the way she has for six years. *Ciao bella. A presto.* I say *a presto* back. I will see her Tuesday. Marsh I will not see again because there will not be a Marsh to see.

He stays on his mat with his forearms on his knees. He is doing the thing men of his type do when they want to be approached without doing the approaching. I let him sit. I close the studio's far windows. I move the candles. I am, when I turn back to him, smiling the small smile I have refined across centuries.

"That was extraordinary," he says.

"Thank you."

He stands. He is taller than I am. He has the kind of jaw that has been told it is a good jaw by enough women to have settled into the certainty of itself. His hands are good hands, soft hands, the hands of a man who has not done physical labor since college.

In a kitchen in Brooklyn Heights two years ago he put his first wife in the hospital. Fractured orbital bone. Three cracked ribs. The matter was settled out of court. The settlement contained a non-disclosure provision. None of this is on the internet. None of it is on his face.

I see it anyway.

The body warms. Under the long black wool I teach in, my breasts have grown heavy, my thighs have gone soft. There is a wetness I did not summon and do not need. The body has its own readiness. The readiness is part of the work.

"Would you like to get a drink?" he says.

"I'd prefer a walk."

His mouth flickers at the corner. He has registered being managed. He has decided he likes it.

—

The vaporetto across to the Zattere is half empty. He stands close to me at the rail. The city's lights are coming up on the far side: the Salute's dome going pink in the last light, the Dogana, the long sweep of the Riva. He talks. He talks about his work — he is the senior fiction editor at one of the older Manhattan houses — and about a weekend in Florence the previous spring, and about a restaurant in Cannaregio where the chef remembered him from a previous visit. He is good at this. He has done it a hundred times.

I make the small responsive noises. *Veramente. Ma certo. Che bello.* My body, beneath the wool, is doing what it does. He is reading the small physical signs and is reading them correctly: she is wet, she is warm, she wants this. He is also reading them wrong, because what he thinks I want is what he is offering. What I want is what I am about to take.

He has an apartment off the Zattere — a friend's, something arranged through someone at the firm, three rooms above a shuttered antiquarian bookseller. He has known me for forty minutes when he says, casually, *mine's closer*, as though this had only now occurred to him.

"All right," I say.

In the entryway he reaches for me before the door is closed. I let him. The door clicks shut. He has me against the wall, his mouth on my neck, his hand under the wool, and I let him because I am older than the script he is following and I know how it ends.

I sink to my knees on the cold tile.

I take him in my mouth without ceremony, without performance, because performance is what he expects and the absence of it is the first currency I pay him. He reads it as hunger. It is not hunger. I do what I have done a thousand times. I do it well. He says my name. He says *God*. He stops saying anything. The tile is cold under my knees. I am thinking, while I do this, about a small Venetian girl from three streets over who I saw this morning carrying a violin case nearly as big as she is. The girl smiled at me when she passed. I smiled back. The body does not need my attention right now. I am elsewhere.

He grips my hair. I pull back before he can finish. I stand. I let him lead me, panting, to the bed.

On the bed he is competent in the way of men who have read about sex and forgettable in the way of men who have only read about it. I let him take me. I watch the ceiling. The ceiling has a small water stain in the corner the renovator missed. He finishes with the small false tenderness men perform afterwards. He says something into my hair I do not register.

I wait ten minutes. I tell him I want to walk down to the water before I go home. He says he'll come. I tell him no, I like the walk alone, I'll text him tomorrow. He believes me. They always believe me. The lie a man wants to be told remains, across centuries, structurally unchanged.

I dress. I let myself out.

—

The Zattere at this hour is empty. The fondamenta runs along the wide black water of the Giudecca canal and the lights of the Redentore are smeared on it like wet paint. I chose this apartment for him, in a sense. I have known he was coming for three days. I have known where he was staying since the moment he walked into my class. I have been doing this work for two thousand years and the work has been refined into an instrument the men I take cannot detect.

I wait.

He comes, as they always come, because the worry catches up — *I should have walked her.* He calls my name from the top of the calle. I turn. I let him cross the fondamenta to me. I let him take my elbow. I let him say the small concerned thing.

Then the wings come out of my back.

It is not a transformation. There is no shimmer, no slow reveal for him to register and resist. The wings are simply there — immense, dark, the span of them wider than the calle — and my hands are no longer hands, and my grip on his shoulders is the grip of something that has been waiting two thousand years for a permitted meal. He has time for one sound. It is not a word.

I lift.

The vertical pull is enormous. I take him straight up past the eaves, past the chimneys, into the cold dark above the canal. He is heavier than the last one. I have been eating well. At sixty meters I stop climbing. I hold him for one second in the dark — his legs kicking at nothing, his face turned up to me, his mouth open in the male incomprehension that *this could be happening to him in particular* — and I let that be the last thing his face does.

I drop him.

I do not watch him fall. I have watched enough of them fall. I turn in the air and beat once, twice, and I am gone over the rooftops toward Dorsoduro, where my own small apartment waits with its own small cat, who will be on the windowsill, who will be hungry, who will not ask where I have been.

The body hits the water at terminal velocity. The Giudecca canal is forty feet deep at that point. By morning he will be a problem for someone in a uniform, eventually for the British consulate, eventually for his second wife in Holland Park.

I land on my own balcony. The wings retract before my feet touch the tile. *Bice* comes through the open window and presses her small white head against my shin. I bend and pick her up. I carry her into the kitchen and open the tin of expensive food, the one with the rabbit, because she has earned it and because I have, tonight, the surplus.

I eat nothing myself. The feeding is done.

I shower. I dry my hair. I get into bed. The sheets are clean — I changed them this morning, because I always change them on the days I know. I lie on my back with my hands at my sides. Within ninety seconds I am asleep, and I sleep, as I always sleep after, without dreaming. The dreams are for the living, and I have, tonight, made one less of those.

I will sleep for nine hours. I will wake at six. I will go to the legatoria downstairs and I will say *buongiorno* to the old binder, *Signor* Pellizzari, and to his granddaughter, Chiara, who looks at me the way she has been looking at me since she was sixteen and has never said anything about it, and I will not yet know that the American writer who will walk into my class tomorrow night will be the thing that breaks two thousand years of careful work.

I will not yet know that the breaking has already begun.

—

## TWO

William Jane has been in Venice three weeks and has written nothing.

He sits in a café on Piazza San Marco watching the puddles from the morning's *acqua alta* drain back into the iron-grated gulleys. Two Americans at the next table are recovering from the flood — a woman trying to repair her ruined boots, her husband on his phone scrolling through work email. He watches them and does not write about them. Writing about Americans in Venice is a thing a man of his profession is supposed to have outgrown.

He has outgrown it. He has outgrown the alternatives. He has not yet replaced them with anything.

His advance is in a bank in New York. His editor — Ed Marsh, whom William has not heard from in nine days — is in a townhouse in Brooklyn Heights. His ex-wife, who took his equity and the house, is in Connecticut. He is forty-six years old. His third novel is fourteen months overdue. He has produced approximately eleven thousand words of which approximately none are usable.

He pays for his coffees. He walks.

He has been walking the city in the late afternoons for three weeks, looking for whatever it is a novelist walks for and pretending, even to himself, that he is only walking. Today he cuts north past the basilica into the residential edges of San Marco — the laundry strung between windows, the small *alimentari* not in the guidebooks. He is looking for something. He is, also, *avoiding* something — the apartment, the laptop, the empty page.

He stops at a *bàcaro* on a calle whose name he does not catch. He orders a small glass of Valpolicella because the afternoon has turned warmer than he expected. He notices, on the way out, a flyer taped to the inside of the window.

The flyer is for a yoga class.

The studio is on the Giudecca — a twenty-minute *vaporetto* ride away, a small inconvenience for someone who does not normally go there. The instructor's name is Lamia. The photograph on the flyer is black and white. She is in a pose he does not know the name of, her arms above her head, her face turned toward a window he cannot see. She is not smiling. She is not doing the thing yoga instructors do in their photographs, which is to perform availability. She is doing something else, and he cannot, looking at the flyer, tell what.

He writes down the address. He writes down the time of the next class.

He does not tell himself why.

—

That night in the rented apartment in San Polo he opens a bottle of Cynar from his suitcase and drinks it on the small balcony watching the bell tower of San Giacomo dell'Orio go dark. He has been looking at the flyer all evening. He has been looking at *her*. He cannot, in any of the readings he has done in his life, place what is doing the work — the angle of her arms, the not-smiling, the something that has caught and is not letting go.

He thinks of his ex-wife once, briefly. He does not allow her to stay.

He thinks of Marsh, who has not answered his emails. Marsh was supposed to be coming to Venice for a meeting two weeks ago. Marsh did not come. Marsh has gone silent. William has been telling himself this is normal — Marsh is busy, Marsh is in the field, Marsh is at home with his family — but the silence has begun to register as something other than busyness.

He thinks of the woman on the flyer.

He sleeps badly. In his dreams he is walking through Venice with someone he cannot see clearly, who is leading him by the wrist down a calle he does not know, and the calle gets narrower and narrower until they have to walk single file, and then the calle ends at a water gate he cannot get through, and the person leading him turns and is, in the way of dreams, both the woman from the flyer and someone he has never seen.

He wakes at three in the morning hard and aching. He does the thing a man does in a rented apartment in a foreign city alone. He keeps his eyes closed. Whatever he is doing, he is not, he tells himself, doing it about the woman on the flyer.

He sleeps after.

In the morning he writes the first three sentences of something that is not a guidebook. He does not yet know if it is a novel. He writes them in the small leather notebook he had brought from New York and not opened.

The sentences are: *She came down the stairs. She did not look at me. I knew, before she reached the bottom, that I was going to ruin my life.*

He closes the notebook. He does not write again that day.

—

## THREE

The body on the rocks at Sant'Elena is found at 6:47 in the morning by a Filipina woman walking her employer's dog. She calls the *Carabinieri*. She does not approach the body. She has seen enough television to know not to.

*Sergente* Tullio Zen arrives at 7:22.

He is sixty-three. He has been in the *Carabinieri* for forty-one years, the last twenty-eight in Castello. He has six years until mandatory retirement. He has a wife in Cannaregio and two grown daughters in Padua. He has, in the back of his work notebook, a list he has been keeping since 1996. The list is the cases he has never closed. The list has been growing.

The body has been in the water for some hours. He does not need the *medico* to tell him this. He has seen enough water-bodies to know how the lagoon's currents leave them — the swelling at the joints, the small blue of the lips, the way the hair lies. The man is American. Mid-forties. Expensive watch still on his wrist, which surprises Tullio: the Adriatic at this stretch is not friendly to drunks who fall in, but it is friendly to the watches of drunks who fall in, and most of those go missing before the bodies turn up.

He notes the watch.

He notes the small specific tearing at the shoulders of the suit jacket, as if the man had been gripped, hard, from above, by something with hands stronger than a person's. The tearing has nothing to do with the water.

He does not write that observation in the report. He keeps it on the list.

The list, by this morning, has six names. The seventh entry is going to be this man, when they identify him, which will not take long — the watch has a custom inscription on the back. *Edward. With love, Constance.* Constance is one search away.

Tullio kneels by the body for a long minute. He does not touch it. He looks at the angle the man's head is at, at the way his hands are positioned, at the small careful details that tell him what kind of death this was.

He has six entries on the list. Three are women, two are men, one is a child — the third entry, from September 2014, a small girl behind Santa Maria Formosa, never found, never closed. The seventh entry, he thinks, walking back to the launch, is going to look like the other three women on the list. Which means he is now in possession of a pattern he had been hoping he was wrong about.

He calls his lieutenant from the launch. He does not, in the call, say *pattern.* He says only: *we may have a problem in Castello.*

His lieutenant, who is fifty-four years old and ten months from retirement, says, *Tullio, we have always had a problem in Castello.*

Tullio does not laugh. He does not say what he is thinking. He says only: *I will come in. I will write the report.*

He hangs up. He stands at the rail of the launch as the boat crosses the bacino, the November sun coming up over the Lido on his left, the bell towers of the city sliding past on his right, and he thinks, as he has thought every morning since 2014: *this city has something in it that does not belong to it.*

He does not yet know that the thing has been here longer than the city.

He does not yet know that the thing teaches yoga on the Giudecca.

## FOUR

William takes the *vaporetto* to the Giudecca on Wednesday evening at five thirty. The boat is mostly empty. He is wearing the only yoga-appropriate clothes he packed, which are gym shorts from his New York gym and a t-shirt from a 5K he ran in 2019 with people he no longer speaks to.

The studio is on the second floor of a converted warehouse two minutes from the *vaporetto* stop. He climbs the wooden stairs. The room at the top is long and dim, with high windows facing the lagoon and the dome of the Redentore behind them. Twelve other people are setting up mats. Eleven women, one man — not him, another man, a Venetian in his sixties who has clearly been coming for years.

He puts down his mat at the back. He has not done yoga in eight years. He is not entirely sure why he is here.

She comes in at five fifty-eight.

He had thought, looking at the flyer for three days, that the photograph had been doing some work — that the woman in the picture had been lit cleverly, that the not-smiling had been a styling choice, that what he had been responding to was an image rather than a person. The woman who walks into the room is not the woman in the photograph. The woman in the photograph was an underestimate.

She is taller than he expected. Mediterranean skin, faintly weathered at the temples. A wide unpainted mouth. Dark eyes set deep, with a downturn at the outer corners that gives her, in repose, the look of a woman who has just received bad news she had been expecting. She is wearing a long black sleeveless tunic over loose dark trousers, and her feet are bare, and her hair is in a loose dark knot at the back of her neck with three undyed strands of grey at her part.

He registers, with a small clean shock, that she is the most beautiful woman he has ever seen and that *beautiful* is not actually the right word for what she is.

She does not look at him.

She greets the class. She greets the older Venetian man, Carlo, by name. She moves down the row of mats, exchanging the small pleasantries with the women. He watches her cross the room. She does not, in any of the crossings, look at him.

The class begins.

He has not been on a mat in eight years. He had thought, walking up the stairs, that this would be embarrassing — that he would be the awkward American flailing at the back while she demonstrated at the front. It is not embarrassing. It is something else. She demonstrates the poses cleanly and simply, in two languages, with an Italian inflection on the Sanskrit that should be funny and somehow is not. He follows. He cannot, on the first pose, hold the position — his shoulders are wrong, his hips are tight, he has been sitting at a writing desk for six months. By the third pose he is sweating. By the fifth pose his shoulders have begun to release in a way they have not released since 2017.

She walks the room as the class works.

She corrects two of the women, lightly, with a hand at the elbow. She corrects Carlo with a word. She approaches William's mat in the fifteenth minute. He is in a forward fold. He cannot see her.

He feels her stop next to him.

He feels her hand at the base of his spine — a single fingertip, three seconds of pressure — and the cue is so clean and so specific that his shoulders drop another inch and his hips rotate one degree forward and he is, suddenly, in the pose his body has been failing to find for fifteen minutes.

She moves on.

She has not said a word to him. She has not looked at him. His body is electric.

He gets through the rest of the class on adrenaline. By the closing *savasana* he is lying on his back with his palms up and his eyes closed and his heart is doing something he has not felt it do in years. He keeps his eyes closed for the full ten minutes. He is, when she brings the class up out of the pose, almost certain he has been crying.

The women roll their mats. The women drift toward the door. He rolls his mat slowly. He does not, on principle, look at her as he is rolling. He is doing the thing the older Venetian man had done — sitting on the mat with his forearms on his knees, performing not-leaving, performing the small theatrical waiting.

She knows. He understands, with the small cold clarity of a man who has begun to register that he is out of his depth, that she has known the entire time he was performing it. The hand at the base of his spine. The not-looking. The way the room had organized itself around the choreography of his arrival, his sweating, his unraveling. He has been managed since he walked in the door.

The room empties.

She walks past him toward the front, closing the high windows, blowing out the candles.

She says, without looking at him: "You came."

He cannot, for a second, speak.

He says: "Yes."

She turns. She is, now, looking at him. The dark eyes are doing the thing the photograph had been doing, which is to ask something he cannot answer. There is, in the dark eyes, a heat that is not the heat of attraction in the way he has experienced attraction before. It is older. It is calmer. It is the heat of something that has been deciding whether he is what it needs.

She says: "Walk with me to the *vaporetto*."

## FIVE

The walk to the *vaporetto* takes eight minutes. They take eleven.

The Giudecca at this hour, in late autumn, is almost empty. The high tide has come and gone and left the *fondamenta* darkened at the edges with the salt-line that I have been watching every November for one hundred and fifty-two years. He walks beside me. He does not speak. He is the kind of man who has learned, somewhere in his career, that silence is a tool, and he is using the tool now because he does not, on examination, know what else to do.

I do not, in the first three minutes of the walk, decide what he is to me.

This is unusual. I usually decide before the second pose of a class. I had decided he was the wrong shape when he was at the door — the heat I run on did not run on him. He was not a meal. He was not a predator. He was something I had not, in two thousand years, met before, and I had given the class with my mind half on his shoulders trying to register what the shape *was*.

I do not, walking him to the *vaporetto*, know what the shape is.

I do know that my body is responding to him in a way my body has not responded to anything in eight hundred years.

Eight hundred years ago, in a small port at the mouth of the Po, I had spent six weeks with a Phoenician trader who had recognized something in me and had not, in the recognition, been afraid. We had been lovers. He had not been a man I needed to eat. He had been a man my body had responded to in a register I had thought I had outgrown. He had died, eventually, of an old man's illness, and I had stood at his bedside, and I had not, after, allowed myself to be in that register again.

Eight hundred years.

I look at the American beside me. He is forty-six, divorced, in Venice on an advance from a publisher whose senior fiction editor is at this moment forty feet under the surface of the bacino. He is the wrong shape. My body is responding to him as if he were the right shape, which is to say as if he were not a shape at all but a person — a person my body would have wanted to be in proximity to even if it had not been hungry, even if it had been fed, even if the part of me that has been the predator since the year four hundred had not been the part doing the choosing.

This is a problem.

He has not, in eight minutes of walking, asked me what we are doing. He has not asked me for my number. He has not asked me about myself.

I say: "Where are you staying."

"San Polo."

"What brought you to Venice."

"A book I am supposed to be writing."

I let the silence work.

He says, eventually: "I am not writing it."

"Why."

"I don't know."

"You know."

He looks at me. The dark eyes, the wide mouth I had been looking at for two minutes on the flyer four days ago, are now ten centimeters from mine. We have stopped walking. We are at the *fondamenta* where the *vaporetto* will come and we are not, either of us, looking at the water.

He says: "I don't know why I came tonight."

"Yes you do."

"All right. I don't want to say it."

"Don't, then."

We stand. The *vaporetto* is two minutes out. The lights of Venice across the water are doing what the lights of Venice across the water do.

I do something I have not done in eight hundred years. I lift my hand and I touch the underside of his jaw with the back of one finger.

He closes his eyes.

The current that should run when I touch a man — the current that tells me *yes, this one, take him* — does not run. There is, instead, a different current, slower and warmer, that runs from my fingertip up my arm and into my chest and settles in a place I have not felt anything settle in since the trader at the mouth of the Po. The current asks me a question I do not have a language for. The question is something like: *what if you did not, this time, eat him.*

I take my hand away.

I say: "Come to the class on Friday."

"All right."

"Bring nothing. Leave nothing. Tell no one."

"All right."

The *vaporetto* is at the *fondamenta*. He boards. He turns once at the rail and looks at me. I do not wave. He does not, either.

I watch the boat pull away.

I stand on the *fondamenta* for a long time. *Bice* will be hungry. The legatoria downstairs will be closing. Chiara — who looks at me the way she has looked at me since she was sixteen and who will, sometime in the next year, do the thing she has been waiting to do — will be at her grandfather's worktable cleaning the brass stamps. I will go home. I will feed the cat. I will not, tonight, eat.

I have not, in two thousand years, gone forty-eight hours without registering my next meal in a city the size of Venice.

I am, tonight, not registering one.

I turn away from the water. I walk the *fondamenta* alone. The salt-line on the buildings is the same salt-line it has been since 1873. The current under the city is the same current. The body I am walking in is the same body.

The body is doing something it has not done in eight hundred years.

The body is, I register with a clean cold clarity, in trouble.

—

## SIX

He goes to the Friday class.

He does not, between Wednesday and Friday, write. He does not, between Wednesday and Friday, drink. He does not, between Wednesday and Friday, read the email from his sister or the email from his agent or the third email from his ex-wife's lawyer about a small remaining matter regarding the equity she did not take.

He walks Venice.

He walks the entire length of the Riva, then through Castello, then back along the Riva, then through San Marco, then over the Accademia bridge into Dorsoduro, then up the long western shore of Dorsoduro to the Zattere. He walks for nine hours on Thursday. He thinks about her for nine hours.

He has, by Thursday evening, made himself a small specific list of things he knows about her:

1. Her name is Lamia.
2. She teaches yoga on the Giudecca twice a week.
3. She is, to his eye, the most beautiful woman he has ever seen.
4. She is not, on examination, *attractive* in the way the women he has been with have been attractive. She is something else. The something else has not, in three weeks of walking Venice and writing nothing, occurred to him as a possibility he was looking for. He had not been looking for it. He had been looking, he had thought, for a *story*.

He suspects, on Thursday evening, that he has found one.

He sleeps badly Thursday night. He dreams the canal-dream again — the narrow calle, the someone leading him, the water gate. This time the person leading him turns at the gate and is, unambiguously, her. She is naked. Her arms are above her head, the way they were in the flyer's pose, and she is laughing at him, and the laugh is not unkind but it is the laugh of someone who has been waiting eight hundred years for him to find his way to the gate.

He wakes hard at four in the morning. He does not, this time, do anything about it. He lies in the dark and looks at the ceiling.

He does not, at four in the morning on a Friday in November, know what he is doing.

He does, at four in the morning on a Friday in November, register that he has stopped doing the small repetitive thing in his head that he has been doing for fifteen years.

The thing in his head has a name. He has not used the name in two and a half decades — he has not, even with his ex-wife, used the name — but the thing has been with him since he was twenty-one. The thing is the loops. The loops are the small recurring scripts the mind runs when the mind is its own enemy. He has, since he was twenty-one, been running the loops or running from them. The marriage, in the end, had been a long version of running from them. The drinking, the writing, the small careful careful attentiveness to other people that had been his particular professional success, had all been versions of the same management.

The loops, since Wednesday night at the *vaporetto* stop, have stopped.

He has not, in the thirty-six hours since she touched the underside of his jaw, run a single loop.

He registers this at four in the morning with a small cold clarity that is not unlike the clarity she had, on the *fondamenta*, registered going through her own body. He understands that the not-running-the-loops is the most consequential thing that has happened to him since he was twenty-one. He understands that the not-running depends on her presence. He understands that this is something he is not going to be able to give up.

He understands, lying in the dark at four-fifteen in the morning, that he has just been told something true about himself for which he has no procedure.

He sleeps for two more hours. He wakes. He showers. He goes to the Friday class.

She is at the door when he comes up the stairs.

She looks at him.

She says, in a voice so quiet only he hears it: "Stay after."

He nods.

He does the class on autopilot. He does not, this time, sweat the way he sweated on Wednesday. The body has, in the forty-eight hours since the *vaporetto* stop, begun to learn what she is teaching it. The shoulders are different. The hips are different. He moves through the poses in a way that is not, quite, his own, and he registers that what he is moving through is not quite a yoga class.

The class ends. The class disperses.

He sits on his mat with his forearms on his knees and he does not, this time, perform the not-leaving. He waits.

She closes the door behind the last woman.

She crosses the room.

She kneels on the mat in front of him.

She puts her hands on either side of his face.

She kisses him.
